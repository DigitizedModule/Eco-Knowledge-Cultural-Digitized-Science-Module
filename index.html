LESSON 1
Effects of Marine and Coastal Processes

The coast is one of the most active parts of the Earth's surface. In the Philippines, many people live near coastal areas and depend on mangroves, seagrass, beach forests, and coral reefs for food and protection. Local fishers and elders know that mangroves help reduce the impact of strong waves and storms, while coral reefs serve as homes for many fish.

Coastal processes such as waves, tides, and erosion continuously shape the land and sea. These are natural events, but when disturbed by human activities, they can lead to hazards like coastal flooding and storm surges. Knowing both scientific facts and local observations helps communities better understand and protect their coastal environment.

Revisiting Marine and Coastal Processes (LEK-Integrated)

Have you noticed the terms in the previous activity? They all describe marine and coastal processes. The science that studies these processes in our oceans and seas is called oceanography. It combines knowledge from physics, chemistry, biology, geology, meteorology, mathematics, and even social sciences to explain the connection between people and the sea.

For coastal communities in the Philippines, these processes are not just scientific terms but part of daily life. Fishers observe tides to know the best time to go out to sea. Elders recall strong waves and storm surges that reshaped their shorelines. Farmers in coastal barangays notice sea level rise slowly affecting rice fields and wells. Earthquakes and crustal movements are remembered for causing tsunamis in some areas.

What's New
Coastal Erosion (LEK in Siargao)

Coastal erosion is the loss or wearing away of land along the shoreline due to the action of waves, tides, currents, strong winds, and storms. In Siargao, residents observe how shorelines slowly change after strong typhoons. Locals recall that during Typhoon Odette (Rai, 2021), many parts of the coast were eaten away by big waves, washing out coconut trees and damaging houses near the shore.

Fisherfolk also notice that habagat winds and monsoon waves gradually erode beaches, especially in areas with fewer mangroves or beach forests. In contrast, elders share that places with healthy mangroves and seagrass beds are better protected because their roots and thick vegetation help trap sand and lessen wave impact.
What's New
Saltwater Intrusion

Saltwater intrusion happens when seawater seeps into underground freshwater sources, called aquifers. These aquifers are made of sand, gravel, or rock that hold groundwater, which people usually get through wells. When saltwater mixes with this groundwater, the water becomes too salty to drink or use for farming. Once an aquifer is contaminated, it can take years, even centuries, to recover.

In Siargao, many families in coastal barangays rely on shallow wells for drinking water. Locals have noticed that after strong typhoons or during long dry seasons, their well water sometimes turns salty. Elders explain that this often happens when the sea pushes farther inland or when too much freshwater is pumped from wells, allowing seawater to take its place. Farmers also observe that saltier soil near the shore makes it harder to grow rice, coconuts, or root crops.
What's New
Submersion (LEK in Siargao)

Submersion is the movement of sand and sediments from the beach into the shallow sea. In Siargao, locals notice this after strong waves and typhoons, when parts of the beach disappear underwater. Elders share that some shorelines slowly sink or change shape, especially in areas without mangroves or beach forests that usually help hold the sand in place.

Mitigation to Coastal Processes and Hazards
Coastal areas have long been preferred places for settlement because they offer rich fishing grounds, fertile land for farming, and easy access for trade and transport. Many coastal communities, especially in the Philippines, have developed traditional ways of living that are closely tied to the sea‚Äîsuch as fishing seasons guided by the moon and local wind patterns, or farming methods that adjust to the tides. These forms of local ecological knowledge have helped communities thrive and adapt to changing coastal conditions. However, as population and development continue to rise in these areas‚Äînow home to about 1.2 billion people worldwide, and expected to reach up to 5.2 billion by the 2080s‚Äîthe pressure on coastal ecosystems and traditional practices also increases. With growing infrastructure and urban expansion, maintaining balance between development and the preservation of local knowledge becomes more important than ever.
The characteristics of coastal environments, however, bring great challenges to human habitation. Coastlines are dynamic systems where land, sea, and air interact, causing continuous changes in shape and stability. Through generations, many coastal communities have learned to observe and interpret these natural signs‚Äîsuch as changes in wave patterns, wind direction, and the behavior of marine species‚Äîto anticipate storms or erosion. This local ecological knowledge has helped them adapt to their environment and reduce risks. Yet, in many places, modern development has overlooked these traditional insights. As a result, communities have suffered from disasters linked to coastal hazards like storm surges and flooding. Today, coastal development often continues with limited consideration of these risks, even as climate change intensifies them. Moreover, human activities such as overfishing, mangrove clearing, and land reclamation disturb the natural balance of coastal ecosystems, threatening both biodiversity and local livelihoods that depend on them.
According to the Department of Environment and Natural Resources (DENR), the coastal ecosystems of the Philippines are among the most productive in the world and hold vast natural resources. They provide food, livelihood, and cultural identity to many Filipinos, especially those who depend on fishing, seaweed farming, and mangrove gathering. Local communities have long practiced traditional ways of managing these resources‚Äîsuch as seasonal fishing, mangrove replanting, and the protection of breeding areas‚Äîto sustain their environment. However, these same coastal zones are also the most geologically active on Earth. Natural processes such as erosion, submersion, and saltwater intrusion often occur here, bringing potential hazards to both people and their surroundings. These effects may lead to the following hazards:
1. Barrier Island Movement
Barrier islands form from sand deposits that protect the mainland from strong waves and serve as habitats for marine life. Over time, erosion and storms can move or destroy them. Local fishers often observe these changes through shifting sandbars and wave patterns, showing their knowledge of the coast‚Äôs natural behavior.

2. Flooding
Flooding damages habitats and causes erosion. Locals often link it to rising sea levels and stronger tides. Through experience, they observe signs like higher high tides or saltwater reaching farther inland‚Äîtraditional knowledge that helps them prepare for climate-related changes.

3. Freshwater Contamination
Saltwater intrusion can mix with groundwater, making wells and drinking sources unsafe. Locals often notice this when their water tastes salty or crops near the shore start to wilt. They link it to overpumping and land changes, showing how local knowledge helps detect early signs of contamination.

What is It
Coastal hazard-prone areas in the Philippines, especially those identified by local government units as highly susceptible to landslides and flooding, are encouraged to relocate residents to safer places. This is most important during typhoons that often bring storm surges and strong waves. While some coastal hazards are natural, local people have observed that certain human activities‚Äîsuch as cutting mangroves, improper waste disposal, and building too close to the shore‚Äîmake these hazards worse.

Through community experience and traditional knowledge, locals have learned simple but effective ways to protect coastal zones, such as replanting mangroves, building bamboo or stone barriers, and observing early signs of flooding from changes in tides and wind direction. These local practices, combined with scientific methods, strengthen mitigation efforts. Mitigation means taking actions that reduce or prevent the long-term risks and damages caused by coastal hazards.

What is It
Coastal ecosystems are rich in natural resources and provide many people with food and livelihood, especially in fishing and tourism. Because of their beauty, many businesses develop near the shore, but this often disturbs the natural balance. Locals have long observed that when mangroves are cut or sand is taken from the beach, erosion and saltwater intrusion become worse.

Erosion happens when strong waves and storms remove sand and soil from the coast. To protect their communities, locals and the government build seawalls, groins, or breakwaters. However, these can sometimes harm natural habitats and prevent sand from returning to the shore. Many coastal communities now combine traditional practices like replanting mangroves and using bamboo barriers with scientific methods to lessen erosion and flooding.
What is It
Global Warming and Coastal Processes

Global warming affects coastal processes such as sea level rise and erosion. When the Earth‚Äôs temperature increases, ice from the polar regions melts, causing the sea to rise. This leads to stronger waves, frequent flooding, and saltwater intrusion in coastal areas.

In many coastal communities in the Philippines, locals have noticed that tides are getting higher and storms are becoming stronger than in the past. These changes damage homes, crops, and fishing areas. Through experience, people have learned to adapt‚Äîby building raised houses, planting mangroves, and joining community cleanups to protect the coast. These local practices, combined with scientific actions, help lessen the effects of global warming on coastal environments.
